How Do We Handle Phenotypic Complexity?

How does one measure something they cannot describe? This is what my research intends to do. We live in an age where machine-learning methods are becoming more and more powerful, and are capable of many tasks from describing images to rediscovering scientific laws. However, the field of evolutionary biology has been a bit behind on this front. For over a decade, there have been more and more calls to take advantage of the "Big Data" that is available in modern times. Now that this data is ubiquitous and large-scale computing power is commonplace, its time to not only rethink how we interact with data, but also how we can use data to rethink the process of discovery itself.

Simulating Raw Data

Many machine learning methods, such as neural networks, operate as "Black Boxes", making it difficult to understand their results and interpret their decisions. This issue is particularly problematic when trying to automatically construct biological traits, which are themselves extremely complex, and are often constructed and used differently by different researchers.

When developing more traditional methods for studying evolution, it is common to test these models on artificial data simulated under a known evolutionary process, such as Brownian Motion. To help assess machine learning models that operate on raw data such as images, I've developed TraitBlender, a Blender addon and workflow for simulating raw data under known processes. Simply put, a researcher can import tabular data simulated in R or Python, map it to a function for simulating a 3D object, and then render museum-specimen style images for every species present in the tabular data, creating an entire image dataset of taxa that do not actually exist, but whos morphological traits are well-defined and whos evolutionary history is perfectly documented.
